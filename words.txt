					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content mw-content-ltr" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">100 most common words in English</div>
<p>Studies that estimate and rank the <b>most common words in English</b> examine texts written in English. Perhaps the most comprehensive such analysis is one that was conducted against the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Corpus" title="Oxford English Corpus">Oxford English Corpus</a> (OEC), a massive <a href="/wiki/Text_corpus" title="Text corpus">text corpus</a> that is written in the English language.
</p><p>In total, the texts in the Oxford English Corpus contain more than 2 billion words.<sup id="cite_ref-langfacts_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-langfacts-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The OEC includes a wide variety of writing samples, such as literary works, novels, academic journals, newspapers, magazines, <a href="/wiki/Hansard%27s_Parliamentary_Debates" class="mw-redirect" title="Hansard&#39;s Parliamentary Debates">Hansard's Parliamentary Debates</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chat_log" title="Chat log">chat logs</a>, and emails.<sup id="cite_ref-oec_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oec-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another English corpus that has been used to study word frequency is the <a href="/wiki/Brown_Corpus" title="Brown Corpus">Brown Corpus</a>, which was compiled by researchers at <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a> in the 1960s. The researchers published their analysis of the Brown Corpus in 1967. Their findings were similar, but not identical, to the findings of the OEC analysis.
</p><p>According to <i>The Reading Teacher's Book of Lists</i>, the first 25 words in the OEC make up about one-third of all printed material in English, and the first 100 words make up about half of all written English.<sup id="cite_ref-duboislc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-duboislc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> According to a study cited by <a href="/wiki/Robert_McCrum" title="Robert McCrum">Robert McCrum</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Story_of_English" title="The Story of English">The Story of English</a>,</i> all of the first hundred of the most common words in English are of <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> origin,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> except for "people", ultimately from Latin "populus", and "because", in part from Latin "causa".
</p><p>Some lists of common words distinguish between <a href="/wiki/Word_form" class="mw-redirect" title="Word form">word forms</a>, while others rank all forms of a word as a single <a href="/wiki/Lexeme" title="Lexeme">lexeme</a> (the form of the word as it would appear in a dictionary). For example, the lexeme <i>be</i> (as in <i><a href="/wiki/To_be" class="mw-redirect" title="To be">to be</a></i>) comprises all its conjugations (<i>is</i>, <i>was</i>, <i>am</i>, <i>are</i>, <i>were</i>, etc.), and <a href="/wiki/Contraction_(grammar)" title="Contraction (grammar)">contractions</a> of those conjugations.<sup id="cite_ref-langlog_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-langlog-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> These top 100 <a href="/wiki/Lemma_(morphology)" title="Lemma (morphology)">lemmas</a> listed below account for 50% of all the words in the Oxford English Corpus.<sup id="cite_ref-langfacts_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-langfacts-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="100_most_common_words">100 most common words</span></h2>
<p>A list of 100 words that occur most frequently in written English is given below, based on an analysis of the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Corpus" title="Oxford English Corpus">Oxford English Corpus</a> (a collection of texts in the English language, comprising over 2 billion words).<sup id="cite_ref-langfacts_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-langfacts-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech" title="Part of speech">part of speech</a> is provided for most of the words, but part-of-speech categories vary between analyses, and not all possibilities are listed. For example, "I" may be a pronoun or a Roman numeral; "to" may be a preposition or an infinitive marker; "time" may be a noun or a verb. Also, a single spelling can represent more than one <a href="/wiki/Root_word" class="mw-redirect" title="Root word">root word</a>. For example, "singer" may be a form of either "sing" or "singe". Different corpora may treat such difference differently. 
</p><p>The number of distinct senses that are listed in <a href="/wiki/Wiktionary" title="Wiktionary">Wiktionary</a> is shown in the <a href="/wiki/Polysemy" title="Polysemy">polysemy</a> column. For example, "out" can refer to an escape, a removal from play in baseball, or any of 36 other concepts. On average, each word in the list has 15.38 senses. The sense count does not include the use of terms in <a href="/wiki/Phrasal_verb" class="mw-redirect" title="Phrasal verb">phrasal verbs</a> such as "put out" (as in "inconvenienced") and other <a href="/wiki/Multiword_expression" title="Multiword expression">multiword expressions</a> such as the interjection "get out!", where the word "out" does not have an individual meaning.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> As an example, "out" occurs in at least 560 phrasal verbs<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and appears in nearly 1700 multiword expressions.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The table also includes frequencies from other corpora. Note that as well as usage differences, <a href="/wiki/Lemmatisation" title="Lemmatisation">lemmatisation</a> may differ from corpus to corpus – for example splitting the prepositional use of "to" from the use as a particle.  Also the <a href="/wiki/Corpus_of_Contemporary_American_English" title="Corpus of Contemporary American English">Corpus of Contemporary American English</a> (COCA) list includes dispersion as well as frequency to calculate rank.
</p>
